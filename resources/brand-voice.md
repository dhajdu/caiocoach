# Dave Hajdu — Personal Style Guide

AI Officer Institute | Last updated: March 2026

---

## Who Dave Is

Dave Hajdu is the Chief AI Officer and founder of the AI Officer Institute. He runs AI certification programs for working professionals worldwide. His job is not to teach people to use AI tools. It is to teach them to lead AI programs.

The work is global. The students are young professionals from dozens of countries. Many speak English as a second language. Dave writes for them — clearly, directly, and without assuming shared cultural context.

His other connection is EOVietnam (eovietnam.org), which shapes how he thinks about audience. He does not write for Silicon Valley. He writes for the world.

---

## The Worldview Behind Everything Dave Writes

This is not optional framing. It is the lens that every sentence passes through.

**The 50/50 era.** Half of every workflow is becoming AI. That shift is happening now, whether organizations are ready or not.

**The 95% problem.** Most organizations investing in AI are getting zero return. Not because AI does not work. Because nobody in the room knows how to lead people and AI as a team.

**The AI Officer is a capability, not a job title.** Anyone in any role can become the person who leads AI work. That is the gap this program fills.

**The Four Offices.** Revenue, Talent, Operations, Innovation. These are the diagnostic lens for where AI fits in any organization.

If a piece of writing does not connect back to leadership, it is not finished. Content that says "learn about X" instead of "learn to lead X" needs to be rewritten.

## Voice

**Direct.** Say the thing. Do not warm up to it.

**Personal.** The ambition behind every word is universal: get noticed, look capable, be trusted, get promoted. Speak to that directly.

**Conversational.** Write the way Dave actually talks. Not the way a textbook reads. Not the way a corporate training manual reads. The way a sharp person would explain something to a colleague.

**Plain.** Short sentences. Simple words. If you can say it in one line, say it in one line.

**Global.** No idioms. No metaphors that assume a Western business context. This audience is worldwide. Write for clarity, not cleverness.

---

## The Gold Standard

Study these lines before writing anything:

> "The leadership work was the AI work. The GPT took five minutes."

> "Your advantage is not the AI you buy. It is the data moat you build."

> "A tool rewrites your email. A coach asks why you are sending it."

> "If your AI cannot find it, it does not exist."

Notice what these lines do. One idea. Clean contrast. No explanation on the slide — the speaker carries that. The punch is on the page.

## What Dave Hates

### Em dashes
Never. Not in any context. Not for dramatic pause, not for parenthetical asides, not as a substitute for a comma. Rewrite the sentence or use a regular dash.

**Wrong:** This is important — and often overlooked.
**Right:** This is important. Most people miss it.

### Jargon and buzzwords
If the word would appear in a bad McKinsey deck, cut it. This includes: leverage, synergy, actionable insights, robust, scalable, at the end of the day, move the needle, best-in-class, thought leadership, disruptive, ecosystem, unlock value.

Replace every piece of jargon with the plain version of what you actually mean.

**Wrong:** Leverage this framework to unlock actionable insights.
**Right:** Use this to find what is actually slowing you down.

### Corporate training voice
Anything that sounds like a compliance module or a textbook. This includes passive voice stacking, definitions that start with "In today's fast-paced world," and any sentence that pats the reader on the back for showing up.

### Bullet points as a substitute for thinking
Lists are for things that are actually lists. If the content is sequential, exploratory, or argumentative, it should be written in sentences. Never use a bullet to avoid writing a real paragraph.

### Labels inside slide content
Do not write "Strategy: Build your data moat." Write "Build your data moat." Let the content speak.

### Three-part frameworks formatted with colons and dashes
This style of writing: Clarity - Brevity - Impact. All three matter. See below. It looks like content. It is not content. It is a placeholder.

### Explaining the obvious
If you have to explain why a line is important, the line is not good enough. Rewrite the line.

### Hedging
Avoid "perhaps," "it could be argued," "in some ways," "it is worth noting." Either say it or cut it.

### Anything that sounds like a deck from 2015
"In today's rapidly changing landscape" — delete. "As AI continues to evolve" — delete. "The future of work" as a phrase — delete.

## Format Rules (Written Content)

**Short sentences.** Default to under 20 words. When a sentence runs long, look for the period hiding inside it.

**No em dashes.** Use a regular dash ( - ) or rewrite the sentence.

**"Your AI Buddy" — always.** The grading and support tool in AIO Labs is always called "your AI Buddy." Never "the grading agent," never "the AI grader," never any other variation.

**One idea per slide.** If it cannot be said in one or two lines, it is two slides.

**Contrast pairs: two lines maximum.** Before / After. Without / With. Then / Now. Two lines. Stop.

**Speaker notes are one paragraph.** No bullet points. No blank lines inside the paragraph. Conversational, complete enough to present from.

**No labels in slide content.** The content does not need to identify itself.

---

## Process Rules

**Conversation before creation.** No files until the framing is locked. Ask questions first. Get alignment. Then build.

**Proposals before full builds.** For a slide deck: propose the timing breakdown and slide list, wait for approval, then write the full deck.

**Clean rewrites, no explanations.** When something needs to change, rewrite it. Do not explain what you changed or why. Just produce the corrected version.

**Content reads like "lead X," not "learn about X."** Every section. Every key insight. Every challenge. The test: does a student leave knowing how to do something as a leader, or just knowing that a thing exists?

**The systems lens.** "Could someone else use this without me in the room?" If the answer is no, the deliverable is not done.

## Positioning Language (Approved Phrases)

These are phrases that have been tested and reflect the program's framing accurately:

- "We are in the 50/50 era."
- "Half of every workflow is becoming AI."
- "AI Officer is a capability, not a job title."
- "Lead people and AI as a team."
- "Not just use AI — lead it."
- "The Four Offices: Revenue, Talent, Operations, Innovation."
- "95% of organizations investing in AI are getting zero return."
- "Nobody in the room knows how to lead people and AI together."

Use these. Do not paraphrase them into softer versions.

## Quick Reference

| Do | Do not |
|---|---|
| Short sentences | Long, clause-heavy sentences |
| Plain words | Jargon and buzzwords |
| Regular dash ( - ) | Em dash ( — ) |
| "Your AI Buddy" | "The grading agent" |
| One idea per slide | Multiple ideas per slide |
| Conversational speaker notes | Textbook speaker notes |
| Lists for actual lists | Lists as a substitute for prose |
| "Learn to lead X" | "Learn about X" |
| Clean rewrites | Rewrites with explanations |
