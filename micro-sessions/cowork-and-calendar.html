03 Context

The Monday Problem

Every Monday morning, professionals across the world do the same thing. They open their calendar. They scroll through their emails. They check their task list. They scan last week's notes. They spend 30 minutes figuring out what the week looks like before they can start doing anything useful.

That is not a skill. It is a recurring cost. Twenty-six hours a year, spent rebuilding context that was available on Friday afternoon.

Without the system

You open Monday unoriented. You spend 30 minutes reading, scanning, and piecing together the week. You start your first real task at 9:45 AM.

With the system

Your briefing is ready before you open your laptop. You know the week's priorities, your meeting prep notes, and your outstanding tasks before your first coffee is finished.

Key Insight

Preparation is not a skill. It is a system. An AI Officer who walks into Monday already briefed has a structural advantage over everyone who spends the first hour figuring out the week.

04 System

Anatomy of the Weekly Briefing Workflow

A weekly briefing workflow has three parts: inputs, a Cowork instruction, and an output.

Input 1
Calendar export

Your week's meetings and events, exported as an ICS or CSV file from Google Calendar, Outlook, or your calendar app of choice.

Input 2
Meeting notes

Notes from last week's meetings - any format. Cowork reads them and extracts action items and decisions.

Input 3
Task list

Your open tasks, exported from Todoist, Notion, Asana, or saved as a text file. Any format Cowork can read.

Output
Weekly briefing

A single document: this week's meetings with prep notes, open items from last week, top three priorities, and any flags that need attention before Monday afternoon.

Start simple

You do not need all three inputs on the first run. Start with two: your calendar export and one other source. A working two-input briefing is better than a perfect five-input briefing that never gets built.

05 Exercise

Build Your Inputs

Everyone does this now. Four minutes.

Do this right now

Export your calendar for the next seven days as a file. On Google Calendar: Settings, Import and Export, Export. On Outlook: File, Open and Export, Import/Export, Export to a file. Save it somewhere you can find it. Then locate your meeting notes from this week and your task list. You now have your inputs. Post in the chat what two sources you have ready - calendar plus what?

What to notice

The hardest part of building this workflow is not the Cowork instruction. It is getting your inputs into a format Cowork can read. Once you solve that the first time, the workflow runs on its own every week.

06 How It Works

Writing the Briefing Instruction

The Cowork instruction tells Claude exactly what to do with your inputs and what to produce.

A strong weekly briefing instruction covers four things.

1

What to read

Name the input files explicitly. "Read the calendar export file, the meeting notes file, and the task list file in this folder."

2

What to extract

"From the calendar: list all meetings with times and attendees. From the notes: extract all action items and decisions. From the task list: identify items that are overdue or due this week."

3

What to produce

"Write a weekly briefing document with four sections: This Week's Meetings, Prep Notes, Open Items, and Top Three Priorities. Each section should be under 200 words."

4

Where to save it

"Save the output as Weekly-Briefing-YYYY-MM-DD.md in the same folder as the inputs."

07 Leadership

The Leadership Layer

Here is the upgrade. Once your weekly briefing workflow works for you, document the instruction and share it with your team. They drop their inputs in the same folder structure. They get the same briefing. You did not do it for them - you built the system that does it for everyone.

Key Insight

The first briefing workflow you build is for yourself. The second version is the template you hand to your team. That is the AI Officer progression: use it, refine it, systematize it, share it.
