Learning Guide

Work from your real business. Every prompt in this session asks about your actual organization and your actual role. The more specific your answers, the more useful your roadmap. Don't use a hypothetical.

Mission Briefs
Brief 1 Framework

The Four Offices of the Future

Most organizations try to figure out where AI fits by looking at their existing departments and asking "where can we bolt this on?" That approach produces pilots that don't connect to outcomes.

The Four Offices is a different starting point. It organizes every business outcome into four categories - not by department, but by result. When you look at your business through this lens, the AI opportunities become visible in a way they weren't before.

REVENUE
The job: grow revenue.

Sales, marketing, retention, expansion. Measured by customers won and customers kept.

TALENT
The job: build the team.

Hiring, onboarding, development, performance. Measured by engagement and output.

OPERATIONS
The job: keep the business running.

Processes, systems, reporting, compliance. Measured by cost of non-revenue generating work.

INNOVATION
The job: build what's next.

New products, new markets, new experiments. AI makes room for this by owning what exists.

The 50/50 Era

Leadership used to be 100% about people. The job changed. Half of every workflow is becoming AI. The leaders who figure out how to design and lead a mixed team - human capacity and AI capacity working together - are the ones who will build organizations that outperform. The Four Offices is how you see where the work splits.

Brief 2 Framework

Three Types of AI

Look at your roadmap. The opportunities on that list don't all feel the same. Some seem simple. Some seem complex. Some feel ambitious. That intuition is right - and there's a framework for it.

Without This Framework

Every opportunity looks the same. You pick the most impressive-sounding one and start building. Three months later you have nothing to show and no idea why.

With This Framework

You know exactly what you're building before you start. You pick the right tools, set the right timeline, and know whether you need engineering support.

PACKAGED AI
A prompt that works every time.
+
Anyone can run it. No guidance needed.
+
No engineering required. Build it today.
+
Foundation for everything else. You earn your way up.
AUTOMATED WORKFLOW
Steps connected and running without you.
+
Trigger fires it. Nobody presses go.
+
No-code tools. Make, Zapier, n8n.
+
Human stays in loop. At the right moments.
AGENTIC WORKFLOW
AI that acts, decides, and escalates without being asked.
!
Requires a proven workflow underneath it. You can't build agents on undocumented processes.
!
Requires clear decision logic. AI needs to know when to act and when to escalate.
!
Sessions 4 and 5 territory. Earn your way here.

Brief 3 Framework

The 5D Framework

Every AI program in this series follows the same five-step pattern. Skip a step and you'll feel it three sessions later.

THE 5D FRAMEWORK
Your blueprint for every AI program you build.
D
Define - Who feels the problem? What does it cost? What does success look like? Today.
D
Discover - What data does AI need? Where does it live? Is it clean? Today.
D
Design - What does the workflow look like? Where does AI fit? Session 3.
D
Determine - What does success look like as a measurable outcome? Session 4.
D
Deploy - How do you train people, set guardrails, and improve over time? Session 5-6.

Define: The Most Important Step

Define is where most AI programs fail - not because people do it badly, but because they skip it. They have an idea, they open a tool, they start building. Three months later they can't explain what the program was supposed to do.

A strong problem statement answers four things:

  • Who specifically feels this problem? Not "the team." Which role? How often?
  • What does it cost? In hours per week, in dollars, in missed opportunities.
  • Why now? What has changed that makes solving this possible or urgent?
  • What does success look like? If this works, what is measurably different?

Worked Example

Jordan's four-line problem statement for resume screening:

Jordan's Problem Statement

"Hiring managers spend an average of 3 hours per open role screening resumes before interviewing. At 30 open roles per year and $80/hr, that's $7,200 in annual screening time. AI-assisted screening could reduce this to 30 minutes per role - saving 75% of the time and $5,400 annually. With application volume up 40% since last year, this is the right time to build this."

Brief 4 Framework

FAST Goals and ROI

SMART goals were built for work that doesn't change much between when you set the goal and when you review it. AI tools improve weekly. What seemed ambitious in January is trivially easy by March.

FAST GOALS
Built for the speed AI moves at.
F
Frequently discussed. Reviewed weekly. Not annually.
A
Ambitious. Push past what feels comfortable. Conservative goals produce conservative results.
S
Specific. A real number you can track. Not "improve" - a measurable target.
T
Transparent. Visible to your whole team. When everyone sees it, everyone owns it.

Four Ways AI Creates ROI

TIME SAVED

Hours to minutes. Multiply by hourly cost. You have a number.

COST REDUCED

Fewer vendors. Less rework. Direct spend that goes away.

QUALITY IMPROVED

More consistent, accurate, professional. Harder to quantify. Often the most valuable over time.

SPEED INCREASED

Respond faster. Close faster. Deliver faster. Time-to-outcome is what leadership watches.

Weak vs. Strong

Weak Goal

"Reduce time spent on resume screening."

No number. No timeline. No accountability. Nothing to prove.

Strong FAST Goal

"Cut resume screening from 3 hours per role to 45 minutes by Session 4. Review weekly. ROI target: $5,400/year."

Specific. Ambitious. Reviewable weekly. Provable.

Your Turn

Want to learn more first? Ask your AI Buddy: "Show me how to write a FAST goal for an AI program with examples from [your industry]."
AI Prompt - Brief 4
Help me write one FAST goal for my AI program.

Here is my problem statement: [paste your four-line problem statement]

A FAST goal is: Frequently discussed (weekly), Ambitious (not 10%),
Specific (a real number), Transparent (share with your team).

First ask me: what is the baseline today - how long does this take
or what does it cost? Then help me set an ambitious but credible target.
Push back if my number is too conservative.

Write the final goal in two sentences. Include a measurable outcome,
a timeline, and the ROI value.
Key Insight

You cannot prove an AI program worked if you didn't define what "worked" means before you started. The goal you write today is the thing you'll point to in Session 6 when you say: here's what we promised, here's what we delivered. If you can't measure it, it won't get funded. And it won't get scaled.

Key Takeaways

  • The job is 50% people. 50% AI. Most leaders have only been trained for one half. The AI Officer is trained for both. This series builds that capability.
  • Every AI opportunity lives in one of four offices. Revenue, Talent, Operations, Innovation. The diagnostic scan takes 20 minutes. Most organizations have never done it.
  • Name the type before you build. Packaged AI, Automated Workflow, Agentic Workflow. The answer changes your tools, your timeline, and whether you need engineering support.
  • Define the problem before you touch a tool. Who feels it, what it costs, why now, what success looks like. Four lines. A number. The foundation everything else gets built on.
  • A FAST goal is not a formality. It's the thing that gets budget approved and programs scaled. If you can't put a number on it, you can't prove it worked.
  • Start simple. Earn your way to agents. The organizations getting real ROI are not the ones with the most sophisticated AI. They built something simple, proved it worked, and scaled.

Lesson 10 Quiz

Checkpoint

Test your understanding before moving to Session 2.

Question 1

A colleague says "let's use AI to improve our marketing." As an AI Officer, what is your first response?

A) Ask which tools they want to use
B) Ask what specific business problem in which Office they're trying to solve
C) Build a prototype to show them what's possible
D) Set a SMART goal for the initiative

Answer

B. The Four Offices lens always comes before the technology. You need to know which Office this belongs to and what the specific problem is before any other conversation.

Question 2

Jordan has 7 opportunities. She wants to start with a full agentic workflow for onboarding. What is the risk?

A) Agentic workflows take too long to build
B) She hasn't proven a packaged prompt for any part of the process - agentic workflows require a proven workflow underneath
C) The Talent Office isn't a good place to start
D) She needs engineering support before she can define the problem

Answer

B. Agentic workflows are built on top of documented, proven workflows. Starting with agents before mastering packaged AI is one of the most common reasons AI programs fail.

Question 3

What is the key difference between SMART goals and FAST goals for AI programs?

A) FAST goals don't need to be measurable
B) FAST goals are reviewed annually rather than quarterly
C) FAST goals are reviewed frequently, set ambitiously, and visible to the full team
D) FAST goals only apply to AI programs

Answer

C. The key differences are cadence (weekly review), ambition (pushed past comfortable), and transparency (visible to everyone). These make FAST goals work at the speed AI moves.

Question 4

You write this problem statement: "We want to use AI to help our sales team be more efficient." What is wrong with it?

A) It mentions a department instead of an Office
B) It has no specific person, no cost, no why now, and no definition of success
C) It is too ambitious
D) It should mention a specific AI tool

Answer

B. A strong problem statement has four elements: who specifically feels the pain, what it costs, why now, and what success looks like. This statement has none of them.
