The Data Checklist: Four Things That Make Data AI-Ready

Use this every time you're preparing data for an AI program. If it's not all four, it's not ready.

  1. Consistent. Same format, same labels, same structure throughout. "New York" and "NY" and "new york" are three different things to AI.
  2. Complete. No missing fields where data should exist. Gaps create blind spots AI won't flag.
  3. Labeled. Every column, every category, every field has a clear name. If a human can't tell what it means, AI definitely can't.
  4. Connected. Related data is linked so the story makes sense. Sales data connected to customer feedback connected to product updates is when AI starts seeing patterns you can't.

Brief 1

The Truth About AI Failure

AI isn't failing because of the technology. It's failing because leadership doesn't understand it, workflows aren't documented, and data isn't organized. That's the real story behind the 95% failure rate. And that's the problem the AI Officer is trained to fix.

It fails quietly. Someone buys a tool, plugs in their data, the output looks wrong, they blame the tool, try another tool, same result, give up. The problem was never the tool. It was the data they were feeding it. And nobody in the room was trained to check that first.

The Numbers Don't Lie

Despite $30-40 billion in enterprise investment into GenAI, MIT found that 95% of organizations are getting zero return. Not because AI doesn't work. Because they skipped the data. They bought the Ferrari and filled it with swamp water. The engine is fine. The fuel is the problem.

Three Examples You'll Recognize

A company drops fifty thousand dollars on AI tools. Marketing is excited. But they still can't track campaign ROI because the data feeding the tool is fragmented across five platforms with different naming conventions.

A team builds an AI chatbot for customer service. Customers hate it. The responses are generic, robotic, useless. Why? The chatbot was trained on a messy FAQ document full of outdated answers.

HR uses AI to screen resumes. But it keeps missing great candidates because the training data had inconsistent job titles and departments, so the model learned the wrong patterns.

Same story every time. The tool worked. The data didn't.

Worked Example: What Bad Data Actually Does

In Challenge 1, you'll feed the messy BOLT taste test data straight to AI and ask for an analysis. Watch what comes back.

AI will give you a confident, professional summary. Charts, trends, recommendations. It looks great. But the data has duplicates, inconsistent ratings, missing fields, and a phone number where a score should be. AI doesn't flag any of it. It just works with what you gave it and delivers a polished answer built on garbage.

Now imagine that analysis goes to Dana. She presents it to leadership. Someone asks about the sample size. The numbers don't add up because three respondents appear twice. Someone asks about the rating scale. It's inconsistent because the intern mixed up 1-5 and 1-10. Dana's credibility takes the hit. Yours too.

That's what bad data does. It doesn't break the tool. It breaks the trust.

Key Insight

The AI Officer fixes the fuel first. This is not a technical skill. This is a leadership decision. The root cause of AI failure is not bad technology. It's that nobody in the room was trained to check the data before building the program. Everyone says AI is working, but without clean data, there's no return on investment. That's the gap you're learning to fill.

It means it's your job to bring the context every time. And when you're building AI programs for your organization, you're figuring out what data across your entire operation needs to be organized, cleaned, and ready so that AI can deliver results at scale. That's the difference between using AI and leading it.

Brief 3

Implementation & Safety

You've learned why data matters and how AI processes it. Before you feed AI anything else, you need to know where that data actually goes. This is a governance decision, not a personal preference. When you're leading an AI program, you don't just pick the smartest model. You pick the one that handles your data the way your organization requires.

The Three Tiers

Data Safety Tiers
Know where your data goes
!
Free Tier - Never use for work. Your data is very likely training the model. Your company's data is making someone else's product better.
$
Paid Tier - Minimum for work. Most tools stop using your data for training. Still going through their servers.
E
Enterprise Tier - Where sensitive data belongs. Contracts about handling, storage, and data agreements.

Worked Example: The Settings Audit

Open whatever AI tool you've been using. Go into the settings and find the section on privacy or data.

In ChatGPT, go to Settings > Data Controls. You'll find toggles for "Improve the model for everyone" and options for chat history. In Claude, check Settings > Privacy. In Gemini, look under Activity Controls in your Google account.

If you can't find it, that tells you something. Because if you don't know where your settings are, you definitely don't know what you're sharing. And if you're leading an AI program for your organization, this is the first audit you run: what tools are people using, what tier are they on, and what are their settings.

Key Insight

Data safety is a governance decision, not a personal preference. The AI Officer doesn't just use tools. The AI Officer decides which tools are safe for which data, and sets the standard for the team. This is one of the first things you'll do when leading an AI program: audit the tools, check the tiers, and make the call. If your team is using free tools for work, that stops today.

Brief 4

The Right AI for the Right Job

When you're designing an AI program for your organization, tool selection is not about personal preference. It's about matching the right capabilities to the right outcomes. Forget the marketing and forget the hype. It comes down to four things.

The AI Kingdoms

ChatGPT
The All-Rounder

Funded by Microsoft. Great for writing, daily tasks, and business workflows. The one most people start with.

Gemini
The Producer

Google's unmatched visual data. Veo 3 for video, Nano Banana for images. Deep Google Workspace integration.

Claude
The Thoughtful Pro

Best at writing and coding. Data advantage from the world's largest bookstore and cloud computing platform.

Four Things That Matter

  1. The data behind the model. Follow the money behind the data. Each kingdom has a different data advantage. Match it to your program.
  2. The user interface features. Context window, multilingual, integrations. Start with what your program needs, then find the tool.
  3. The safety controls. Training data opt-out, access management, enterprise agreements, audit trail. If the controls don't fit, the tool doesn't fit.
  4. The apps it integrates with. CRM, project management, email, docs. If it doesn't integrate, you're creating more manual work.

Worked Example: Choosing a Tool for a Program

Imagine you're leading an AI program to automate monthly reporting. Before you pick a tool, run through the four criteria.

Data: Financial data, customer metrics, internal documents. You need a model that handles structured data well. Claude and ChatGPT both work. Gemini is strong if your data lives in Google Sheets.

Interface: Large Excel uploads, formatted outputs, saved custom instructions for consistency month to month.

Safety: Financial data is sensitive. Free tier is out immediately. If reports include salary or revenue data, enterprise tier is the right call.

Integration: Google Workspace team? Gemini is already inside your tools. Slack and Notion? ChatGPT has plugins. Deep document analysis? Claude's project feature.

The answer isn't "which AI is best." The answer is "which AI fits this program."

Key Insight

The AI Officer doesn't ask "what's the best AI?" The AI Officer asks "what's the best AI for this program?" Tool selection is a design decision, not a personal preference. Match the data advantage to the outcome you need. Follow the money behind the data. This same evaluation scales: one tool for one program today, a portfolio of tools across your organization tomorrow.
