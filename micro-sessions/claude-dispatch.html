03 Concept

The Generalist Problem

When you ask one agent to handle every type of request, it approaches all of them with the same logic, the same format, the same assumptions. Which means it handles most of them adequately - and none of them exceptionally.

This is not an AI limitation. It is an instruction design problem.

Generalist agent

Handles research, drafting, scheduling, and data analysis. Does all of them at roughly the same level. Quality is inconsistent. Hard to improve because every change affects all task types.

Specialized agents with dispatch

Each agent handles one type of work with a purpose-built brief. Quality is consistent and improvable. A dispatcher reads every request and routes it to the right specialist automatically.

Key Insight

Specialization beats generalization every time. The question is not whether to specialize - it is how to route work to the right specialist without doing it manually yourself.

04 System

What a Dispatcher Does

A dispatcher is itself an agent. But its role is pure routing. It reads what comes in, categorizes it, and sends it to the right specialist. It does not do the work. It decides who does.

Step 1
Read the request

The dispatcher receives the incoming request - a message, a file, a task description, or a structured input.

Step 2
Categorize it

The dispatcher matches the request against its category list. Is this a content request? A data request? A scheduling request?

Step 3
Route it

The dispatcher sends the request to the specialist agent whose brief matches this category. The specialist does the work.

Step 4
Return the result

The specialist's output comes back. The dispatcher passes it to the right destination - a document, a person, a system.

Key Insight

Separating the decision from the work is one of the cleanest patterns in agent design. The dispatcher decides. The specialist executes. Both become more reliable when they are not trying to do both jobs at once.

05 Framework

Map Your Work First

Before you build a dispatcher, you need to know what it is dispatching. That means knowing your own work categories first.

Think about the last two weeks. What types of requests landed on you? Not the specific tasks - the categories. Most roles have 4 to 6 distinct types.

1

Identify your categories

List the 4 to 6 types of work that come to you most often. Be specific - "content" is not a category. "Draft a client-facing LinkedIn post from a brief" is a category.

2

Name the trigger signals

What words or patterns signal each category? These signals are what your dispatcher will use to route. If you can recognize a request type in 3 seconds, a dispatcher can too - if you tell it how.

3

Define the output

What does a good result look like for each category? The dispatcher needs this to confirm that the specialist produced the right thing.

06 Exercise

Build Your Dispatch Map Live

Everyone does this now. Five minutes.

Do this right now

Open a doc. Write down your 4 to 6 work categories. Next to each one, write the trigger signal - the words or context that tell you this type of request just arrived. Then write one sentence on what a good output from this category looks like. That is your dispatch map draft. Post your categories in the chat when the trainer asks.

What to notice

The trigger signals are usually obvious to you because you have been routing manually for years. You already know what signals what. Writing it down is the first step to making it automatic.

07 Leadership

The Leadership Layer

Manual routing is invisible labor. Every time you decide what goes where, you are doing work that does not show up on a report, does not get recognized, and does not scale.

A dispatch architecture makes that routing explicit, documented, and automatic. When a new team member joins, they do not need to learn how to route work - the dispatcher already knows.

Key Insight

The decision architecture is organizational knowledge made explicit. When you build a dispatch map, you are not just saving yourself routing time. You are documenting how work flows in your organization - and making that flow automatic.
