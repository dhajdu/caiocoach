03 Concept

Before and After

Prompts are sparks. Research is firewood. A quick prompt gets you a fast answer. Research gets you something you can stake your name on.

Quick prompt

"What are 5 AI trends I should be watching?" Fast. Probably accurate in a general sense. But where did it come from? Could you cite it in a presentation? Could you defend it if someone pushed back?

Deep research

Same question, but you point AI to the MIT AI Index and the Stanford HAI report. Now the answer has specific data points, publication dates, and named authors. You can stake your name on it.

Key Insight

AI is 99% accurate when you give it the right data and prompt it the right way. The data is your responsibility. The tool is only as good as the sources you feed it.

04 Framework

The Anatomy of Deep Research

Five steps. Every credible AI research output follows this pattern.

1

Ask a tight question

Small enough to be clear, big enough to matter. Not "what is happening in AI" - "what are the three biggest barriers to AI adoption in mid-sized companies in Southeast Asia?"

2

Gather multiple sources

Point AI to data you trust: academic studies, industry reports, validated datasets. Not random websites. Not news summaries. Primary sources.

3

Read and reflect

Look for contradictions, gaps, and surprises. What do two sources disagree on? What important question is nobody answering? That is where the real insight lives.

4

Pull a thread

Follow your curiosity. When something surprises you, go deeper instead of accepting the first answer. The best insights come from the second or third layer.

5

Turn it into clarity

Aim for insights you would stake your name on. Specific. Cited. Connected to a real decision someone needs to make.

Pro Tip

Treat AI as a research assistant, not a decision-maker. You choose the sources. You judge the quality. You make the call. AI handles the reading and synthesis. That division of labor is what makes the output trustworthy.

05 Tool

NotebookLM

NotebookLM is a research tool from Google that forces you into best practice. You cannot just search the web and trust whatever comes back. You bring your sources, and it works only from what you give it.

That is not a limitation. That is the point.

What you can do with your sources

Upload your source

Add a link, upload a PDF, or connect a Google Drive document. NotebookLM immediately generates a summary and lets you ask questions about the content.

Then go further

Generate a mind map of the document's structure. Create an audio overview - a podcast-style conversation explaining the material. Build a video explainer. All cited to your source.

Key Insight

The mind map feature is underrated. Upload a long academic paper, generate the mind map, and you understand the structure and key findings in five minutes instead of two hours.

Hybrid Workflow

Combine NotebookLM with ChatGPT Projects: upload your key sources into NotebookLM to get summaries and mind maps, then import those insights into a ChatGPT Project to draft blogs, strategy documents, or training decks. Best of both tools.

06 Tools

Tools of the Trade

Best

NotebookLM

Source-driven research. Forces credibility. Mind maps, audio overviews, video explainers. Free tier available. Best tool for deep research workflows right now.

Good

ChatGPT with Deep Research mode

Powerful when pointed at credible sources. Deep Research mode is thorough but slow. Best used when you need synthesis across multiple sources and have time to wait.

Good

ChatGPT Projects

A solid alternative to NotebookLM. Upload your own academic resources directly from the source website. Less polished output features, but same principle: your sources, your control.

Limited

Copilot

Useful if your documents live in Microsoft 365. Good for research within your organization's files. Not a strong choice for external academic research.

07 Example

The BOLT Connection

Dana Reyes needs to pitch BOLT's expansion into three new markets at a leadership meeting on Thursday. She cannot show up with "here are five trends I asked AI about."

Without deep research

Dana asks AI for consumer trends in Jakarta. She gets a general summary. No citations. No data she can defend. Leadership pushes back and she has nothing to stand on.

With deep research

Dana finds three credible market reports, uploads them to NotebookLM, generates a cited summary per market. She walks into Thursday with specific data points and named sources. Leadership approves the expansion.

Key Insight

The difference between being heard and being dismissed in a leadership meeting is often one thing: citations. Deep research is how an AI Officer builds credibility, not just speed.

10 Takeaways

Key Takeaways

  • The data is your responsibility AI is only as good as the sources you feed it. Random internet results produce generic outputs. Credible sources produce insights you can defend.
  • NotebookLM forces the right workflow It will not let you skip the source step. That constraint is the feature. Everything it produces is tied to what you gave it.
  • The mind map is underrated Upload a dense academic paper and generate the mind map. You understand the whole structure in five minutes. That is leverage.
  • Automate your research workflow Once you understand the pattern - tight question, credible sources, cited output - you can build an agent that runs it for you on a schedule. That is what turns a skill into a system.
11 Try This Week

Try This Week

Pick one topic you keep getting asked about at work. Find one real source on it - a journal paper, an industry report, something with a named author or institution. Put it into NotebookLM and generate a mind map.

Then do something with it. Write a short summary. Bring a cited insight to your next team meeting. Draft one paragraph for a report you are working on. The goal is not research for its own sake - it is research that changes something.

Go Further

Upload two or three sources on the same topic and ask NotebookLM to find the contradictions between them. Where do credible sources disagree? That is usually the most important question - and the one most people never ask.
